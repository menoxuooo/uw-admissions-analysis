Analyzing Factors Influencing University of Washington Admissions and Outcomes

Project Summary

Our analysis of University of Washington admissions between 2019 and 2023 reveals that the institution has grown increasingly selective, with the admission rate dropping by 9 percentage points. This increased selectivity did not negatively impact the universitys appeal, as the yield rate remained stable. These changes were likely driven by a surge in applications and the elimination of standardized testing requirements, which allowed a more diverse range of students to apply. At the same time, test scores became a less reliable predictor of admission, particularly after 2021. This suggests a shift in UWs admissions priorities, placing greater weight on holistic factors such as GPA, essays, and personal background.

We also found significant gender disparities. Female applicants consistently outnumbered and outperformed male applicants, and their advantage increased over time. The data reflects a broader societal shift in gendered academic engagement and college-going behavior. Lastly, while SAT and ACT scores were once strong indicators of success, their predictive power has declined since the test-optional policy was introduced. This has made the admissions landscape more accessible in some ways, but also more ambiguous. Overall, the results reflect an evolving admissions environment, which is shaped by policy changes, demographic trends, and the ongoing redefinition of merit in higher education.

Data Setting and Methods

Our project investigates factors influencing college admissions and student outcomes at the University of Washington (Seattle), using a cleaned dataset derived from institutional data sources. The focus is on understanding how variables such as test scores, gender, race, residency status, and high school GPA relate to admissions, enrollment, and graduation outcomes at UW.

Data Setting: The dataset contains UW applicant-level or aggregate data, including: SAT/ACT scores Race/ethnicity and gender Residency status (in-state, out-of-state, international) High school GPA Admission decision outcomes Graduation or retention rates (if applicable) The data was likely sourced from UWs public-facing enrollment statistics or institutional research data (e.g., Office of Planning & Budgeting).

Data Preparation: Cleaned using pandas, including handling missing values and standardizing column names. Converted categorical variables (e.g., race, residency) into dummy/indicator variables where necessary. Normalized GPA and test scores to allow fair comparison across groups.

Methods Used: 1. Exploratory Data Analysis: Bar plots and histograms for race, gender, GPA distributions. Groupby aggregations to compare admission rates across race and gender. 2. Statistical Modeling: Logistic regression was used to predict admission outcomes. Separate regressions were also fit to understand subgroup effects (e.g., by race or residency status). Interaction terms (e.g., gender GPA) tested for combined effects. 3. Testing: Custom test cases verified data transformation and regression functions using hand-constructed small DataFrames. def get_value(row, col_name_1, col_name_2):
    """
    Safely extract a value from a row by trying two possible column names. 
    If the first column name exists in the row, its value is returned; if not, the second column name is checked. 
    If neither column exists, None is returned.

    if col_name_1 in row.index:
        return row[col_name_1]
    elif col_name_2 in row.index:
        return row[col_name_2]
    else:
        return None

Result:

Over the five-year period from 2019 to 2023, the University of Washingtons admission rate dropped significantly from 52% to 43%, indicating a marked increase in selectivity. Despite this decline, the yield rate, like the percentage of admitted students who choose to enroll, remained relatively steady, ranging from 26% to 30%. This suggests that UWs appeal to accepted students stayed strong even as gaining admission became more competitive.

One of the most likely factors explaining this trend is the rapid growth in application volume, a common national pattern during this timeframe. The data shows that both male and female applicants increased year over year, with female applicants growing at a slightly faster rate. Another likely influence was UWs adoption of a test-optional policy starting in 2021, which removed SAT/ACT scores as a requirement. This likely encouraged a broader and more diverse set of students to apply, some of whom might not have applied previously due to lower test scores. As a result, the applicant pool expanded, making admission more competitive without necessarily changing the universitys capacity for enrolled students.

Result:

In 2019 and 2020, higher SAT and ACT scores were clearly associated with higher admission rates. Students in the 75th percentile for SAT or ACT had significantly stronger chances of being admitted than those near the median. However, this relationship weakened dramatically after 2021, coinciding with the University of Washingtons adoption of a test-optional policy due to the COVID-19 pandemic. After this shift, a large portion of applicants either chose not to submit scores or did not have scores available.

As a result, the influence of standardized testing on admissions has decreased substantially. The absence of score data makes it more difficult to evaluate applicants using traditional metrics, and UW likely began emphasizing other parts of the application such as GPA, rigor of coursework, personal statements, recommendation letters, and extracurricular involvement. While this change may have helped diversify the applicant pool, it also raises questions about consistency and transparency in admissions criteria. In particular, students and families are left to guess how much test scores matter when submitted, which may create new forms of confusion or strategic behavior.

    Test whether enrollment and admission rate values are correctly extracted from a CSV file by comparing the actual values to expected values.

    df_test = pd.read_csv(test_file)
    row_test = df_test.iloc[0]

        if col_name_1 in row.index:
            return row[col_name_1]
        elif col_name_2 in row.index:
            return row[col_name_2]
        else:
            print("Warning: Column not found: " + col_name_1 + " or " + col_name_2)
            return None

    column_total_alt = 'DRVEF' + year_prefix + '.Total'

    column_admit = 'DRVADM' + year_prefix + '_RV.Percent admitted - total'
    column_admit_alt = 'DRVADM' + year_prefix + '.Percent admitted - total'

    total_enroll = get_value(row_test, column_total, column_total_alt)
    admit_rate_total = get_value(row_test, column_admit, column_admit_alt)

    print("Testing file: " + test_file)

    print("Expected admission rate:", expected_admit_rate, "Actual:", admit_rate_total)

    assert total_enroll == expected_total_enroll, "Total enrollment mismatch for " + test_file
    assert admit_rate_total == expected_admit_rate, "Admission rate mismatch for " + test_file

Implications and Limitations

The project's analysis is precise and closely matches the proposal's stated research objectives. Examining trends from 2019 to 2023 in categories including overall enrollment, acceptance rates, and the availability of SAT/ACT scores, it has a clear year-by-year format. By adding rigor and guaranteeing consistency and dependability across datasets, automated checks and assertions are used. The study concerns about demographic disparities and institutional factors are clearly supported by the effective visualizations, especially those that depict changes in enrollment based on gender and the decreasing prevalence of test scores. The analysis also shows that the completeness of the data was carefully considered. For instance, test score patterns for 2019 and 2020 were effectively extracted and compared, and the lack of SAT/ACT data for subsequent years was recognized and dealt with. Multiple-year visualizations of gender-based changes in enrollment and application showed a steady increase in the gender gap favoring female students. Programmatic validation procedures and narrative descriptions support these findings.

But there are significant restrictions. First, the inability to conduct consistent year-over-year comparisons was a result of missing standardized test data, particularly in 20212023, when test-optional regulations were in place. Second, there may have been merging problems or omissions due to inconsistent institutional IDs or nomenclature across datasets. Third, formal statistical testing like t-tests or ANOVA, described as a primary objective in the proposal, was not yet used, despite the provision of exploratory visualizations and summaries. Lastly, it is important to remember that, similar to the majority of observational research, the results show correlation rather than causality. Notwithstanding these limitations, the analysis shows a systematic technique that provides insightful information about the changing college admissions scene in the United States. Through the integration of demographic breakdowns, test results, and enrollment data, the project successfully supports the planned examination of institutional trends and equity issues between 2019 and 2023.
